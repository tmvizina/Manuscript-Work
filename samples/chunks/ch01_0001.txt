Chapter 1: The First Broken Seal

The road had begun in a tavern.

Not the road of childhood — that had belonged to Tomas Vale, to rooms left before dawn, names worn thin, and towns abandoned before they could become home. That road had ended in Brindleford, in smoke, in a cart’s shadow, and in DAH-see’s cloak thrown around a boy who had no father left to follow.

Years lay between that boy and the man Gilbert had become. DAH-see had given him work before he gave him lessons, and lessons before he gave him trust. Gilbert learned steel, hunger, watchfulness, and the hard arithmetic of the road. He grew taller. His hands hardened. The name de Vere settled on him until even grief answered to it.

By the second year of his wandering with the company, he had seen cities rot from beneath their own stones. He had watched men with clean hands buy murder by the ounce and measure out betrayal in careful lines of ink. He had heard prophecies spoken in voices that did not belong to the mouths that shaped them, and he had learned, slowly and painfully, that dragons were not the worst things in the world that wore scales.

Yet the beginning remained uncertain.

That troubled him more than he liked to admit.

A man wanted the first step of a road to be clear. He wanted to look back and say, There. There was the place where mud gave way to stone. There was the moment when chance became purpose. There was the last breath of one life, and the first breath of the next.

Memory offered no such kindness.

Memory was not a clerk, dutiful and patient, sorting truth from year to year. Memory was a beggar with a sack full of broken things. It kept what it pleased. It cast away what a man might have sworn he would never forget. A face could remain while a name vanished.
