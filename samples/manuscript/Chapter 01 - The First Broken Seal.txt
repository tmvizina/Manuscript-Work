Chapter 1: The First Broken Seal

The road had begun in a tavern.

Not the road of childhood — that had belonged to Tomas de Vere, to rooms left before dawn, names worn thin, and towns abandoned before they could become home. That road had ended in Brindleford, in smoke, in a cart’s shadow, and in Dossi’s cloak thrown around a boy who had no father left to follow.

Years lay between that boy and the man Gilbert had become. Dossi had given him work before he gave him lessons, and lessons before he gave him trust. Gilbert learned steel, hunger, watchfulness, and the hard arithmetic of the road. He grew taller. His hands hardened. The name de Vere settled on him until even grief answered to it.

By the second year of his wandering with the company, he had seen cities rot from beneath their own stones. He had watched men with clean hands buy murder by the ounce and measure out betrayal in careful lines of ink. He had heard prophecies spoken in voices that did not belong to the mouths that shaped them, and he had learned, slowly and painfully, that dragons were not the worst things in the world that wore scales.

Yet the beginning remained uncertain.

That troubled him more than he liked to admit.

A man wanted the first step of a road to be clear. He wanted to look back and say, There. There was the place where mud gave way to stone. There was the moment when chance became purpose. There was the last breath of one life, and the first breath of the next.

Memory offered no such kindness.

Memory was not a clerk, dutiful and patient, sorting truth from year to year. Memory was a beggar with a sack full of broken things. It kept what it pleased. It cast away what a man might have sworn he would never forget. A face could remain while a name vanished. A smell could linger after a whole conversation had been swallowed by years. A single word might stand sharp and bright in the mind while the road around it faded into mist.

Such was the memory of men.

Such, perhaps, was mercy.

He remembered the tavern clearly enough.

It had been a low, warm place, old in the way that taverns were old, with beams blackened by smoke and tables scarred by knives, dice, mugs, and the restless hands of men with too many stories and too little coin. The rafters trapped a gray-blue haze above the common room. Spilled ale had soaked so deeply into the floorboards that the smell seemed part of the wood itself. Somewhere near the hearth, a pot of stew bubbled with more ambition than meat. Men laughed too loudly. Dice clicked. A chair scraped. A serving girl cursed a wandering hand with such artful precision that half the room applauded and the other half took great care with where they placed their fingers.

Outside, the world had troubles.

Inside, men pretended trouble knew how to wait politely at the door.

Gilbert had arrived with Dossi.

That, too, he remembered.

They had taken the corner table without discussing it. Dossi chose the seat with the wall behind him, as he always did, and Gilbert took the one beside him, close enough to hear a low word, far enough not to look like a boy waiting for instruction. When the serving girl came, Dossi ordered bread enough for two and ale enough for one, then pushed the cup toward Gilbert after taking the first swallow himself.

It was not tenderness.

Dossi would have denied it if accused.

But care, after enough years, learned to disguise itself as habit.

Dossi had been older even then, though not old in the way of bent backs and trembling hands. He was old as a knife was old after many uses; worn, honed, and all the more dangerous because of it. His face seemed made for squinting at bad bargains. His mouth could turn a pleasant word into a warning without changing shape. He had taught Gilbert much, though Gilbert had not always known he was being taught.

Sword-work, yes. Shield-work. Where to put his feet when mud wanted to steal them. How to watch a man’s shoulders rather than his blade. How to hear the lie in a merchant’s price before the lie found words.

But Dossi had taught more than steel.

He remembered names, roads, debts, prices, and obligations. Gilbert did not know then how much of their future would depend on someone stubborn enough to keep account of what others preferred to forget. He knew the difference between a promise and a promise worth believing. He knew which smiles belonged to honest fools, which to dishonest fools, and which to men who had long ago ceased being fools at all. In those days, Gilbert left such matters to him. A young man with a strong arm and a good shield often mistook decisiveness for wisdom. Gilbert had been no different.

Aaric had been with them as well.

Quiet, or quiet enough when silence served. He had sharp eyes and quicker hands, and a past that sat on him like a cloak worn inside out. Some men carried their histories openly, like banners. Some buried them. Aaric’s followed him at a distance, close enough to be seen in the turn of his head, far enough that no decent man could name it without invitation. Even then, there was a mercy in him he kept hidden as carefully as any stolen thing, as though kindness were another weakness waiting to be named.

Gilbert had learned early not to pull too hard at such threads.

He suspected Aaric had been a thief once. Or a criminal. Or perhaps merely a man who had done what he must and been named by people who had the comfort to judge him. From the height of a magistrate’s bench, there was little difference between a thief, a sellsword, and any man with a blade who answered to no lord.

The world named men by who feared them.

That night, the tavern held more than the three of them.

There was a dwarf unlike any Gilbert had ever seen, with skin dark as polished obsidian and a voice that rolled through the room like a barrel kicked down a stair. Oolong Timbernips. That name, at least, memory had not lost. A man would have had an easier time forgetting thunder.

Oolong had bought drinks for men he had never met, accepted praise from men who had not offered it, and laughed as if the whole world were a joke told for his particular enjoyment. His gestures were broad. His smile broader. His manner suggested that any problem might be solved by leaning into it shoulder-first and seeing what gave way.

Yet Gilbert remembered the eyes.

Not clearly. Not in any way he could later say with confidence, without suspecting later knowledge had seeped backward into old recollection, staining it with meanings that had not been there at the time. But there had been something behind Oolong’s noise and swagger. Some hidden depth beneath the laughter. Some glimmer of knowledge, or doom, or merely loneliness so well-armored that it mistook itself for mirth. Gilbert did not yet know how often that laughter would stand guard over fear, knowledge, and doors no sensible man opened lightly.

At the time, Gilbert had thought him strange.

Most beginnings disguised themselves that way.

And there had been Charlie.

A tiefling, though Gilbert had known few enough then to judge one properly. Charlie wore the shape of a man, but his skin held the red of dawn spilled across cloud, and horns rose from his brow in a way no hat could have hidden even had he wished to hide them. He moved easily through glances, whispers, and the quick appraisals of men deciding whether strangeness meant danger. He wore his difference like a cloak he had long ago grown tired of adjusting.

A lute hung from his shoulder, scuffed from use rather than display. He played that night.

Gilbert could not remember the songs.

That failure annoyed him still.

He remembered the bend of Charlie’s fingers upon the strings. He remembered the way the common room changed when music found its footing, how even drunk men lowered their voices, and dice stopped in hands for a heartbeat before falling. He remembered smoke coiling above the tables, the glow of firelight along Charlie’s horns, and the easy grin Charlie gave a man who asked for a song Gilbert could no longer name.

But the songs themselves were gone.

Swallowed by roads. By battles. By blood. By all that came after.

Charlie sold hand-rolled tobacco cigarettes as well, though he gave away enough of them that Gilbert wondered whether the selling was business or merely an excuse to talk. He had a jovial manner, that was the word for it. Jovial. A word too small for what waited inside him, but true enough for that night. For now, when Charlie gathered a room into silence, he did it by choice.

The world had not yet chosen him for the voice that would later speak through his mouth.

Not long after, work found them.

Or they found it.

Gilbert could not remember which, and there lay the trouble of it. He remembered a man from the village, or perhaps a farmer from the outlying fields. He remembered worry in a face, though not the face itself. He remembered Dossi leaning back in his chair, listening in that patient way of his, which made men nervous enough to speak more truth than they had intended. He remembered talk of coin, though not the amount. He remembered thinking it was simple work.

That thought, at least, he remembered clearly.

There had been a scrap of parchment, too, or perhaps only a folded note passed across the table while coin was named. Gilbert remembered cheap brown wax cracked beneath Dossi’s thumb, an ordinary seal breaking with an ordinary sound. Years later, that small sound would trouble him more than the promised pay.

There had been raids near the town. Livestock missing. Travelers harried. Smoke seen where no hearth ought to be. Tracks in soft ground that were not wolf, not man, not anything respectable. Small figures moving at dusk. A child had heard chittering near a well. A shepherd had found blood on stones and no body to match it.

Perhaps someone spoke the name Cult of the Dragon.

Perhaps not.

If they did, Gilbert had not understood the weight of it. Names were only sounds until the world taught a man what they meant. A boy might hear the word war and think of banners. A man who had seen one thought of mud. A fool might hear dragon and think of songs.

Gilbert had been young enough to be more fool than man in some matters.

Ignorance changed nothing.

War did not require a man’s understanding in order to begin. Wheels turned in distant rooms. Knives were sharpened by lamplight. Orders passed from hand to hand. Coin changed purses. Old powers stirred in sleep and called it dreaming.

The first sparks had already fallen into dry grass long before Gilbert knew there was a fire.

So they went.

Gilbert, Dossi, and Aaric left the tavern’s warmth beside Oolong Timbernips and Charlie with the dawn still pale on the road. Or perhaps it had been afternoon. Memory shifted there, as memory did. Gilbert remembered boots in mud, the weight of his shield across his back, Dossi’s bow wrapped against damp, Aaric ranging a little apart from the others, Oolong talking loudly enough to frighten birds from hedges, and Charlie humming under his breath.

Five men walking into a story none of them yet understood.

The trail led them to caverns.

Gilbert remembered the mouth of the place best.

It opened in the side of a low hill like a wound that had refused to heal. Scrub grass bent around it. Stones lay scattered before the entrance like teeth knocked loose from a jaw. The air that breathed from within was cold and wet, smelling of stone, old water, and the sour musk of creatures that lived too long without sunlight.

Dossi crouched near the entrance and touched the ground with two fingers. Aaric watched the dark. Oolong peered in as if darkness might be intimidated by confidence. Charlie said something light enough to earn a grunt from Dossi and a smile from no one.

Gilbert remembered tightening the strap of his shield.

Some older habit, older even than Dossi’s lessons, made his eyes count the dark before his courage had time to speak. Door. Shadow. Narrow place. Too many stones where feet might slip. Tomas de Vere had taught him to fear what a room could hide. Dossi had taught him what to do after fear proved right.

Inside, they found kobolds.

Small things, half a man’s height, with narrow limbs, quick bones, and reptilian hides stretched tight over malice. Their faces were something between dog and dragon, if a dog had learned spite and a dragon had been cheated of grandeur. Alone, one seemed little threat. A farmer’s danger, perhaps. A child’s nightmare. Not a warrior’s.

Together, they were another matter.

They came from cracks and side passages. They skittered over stone where a man had to place his feet with care. They threw stones, spears, curses, and themselves with equal enthusiasm. Their traps were crude, but crude did not mean harmless. A falling rock cared nothing for craftsmanship when it met a skull. A sharpened stake in a covered pit did not ask whether the man falling toward it had noble blood.

Gilbert learned quickly to respect them.

Not fear.

Respect.

There was intelligence in the ambushes, sharper than their hunched forms suggested. One kobold yapped from a ledge while two others crept from the side. A tunnel narrowed just where a shield became awkward. Loose stones gave underfoot where a man might try to charge. They had been taught, or commanded, or frightened into obedience by something larger than themselves.

Oolong laughed when the first sling-stone cracked against the wall near his head, then cursed when the second caught him squarely. Dossi’s bow snapped once, twice, three times in the dark, each arrow finding more than shadow. Aaric vanished into an angle of stone and reappeared where no honest man could have moved so quickly. Charlie fought with a blade then, or perhaps a bow; Gilbert’s memory would not settle on it. He remembered only the flash of red skin in torchlight, the set of Charlie’s jaw when laughter left him, and the strange sight of a musician’s hands doing bloody work with competence.

Gilbert held the front where he could.

That was what he knew then. Put steel between danger and those who had less armor. Keep the shield high. Let fear come later if it must come at all.

The kobolds died badly. Most creatures did.

Yet there were more of them than there should have been, and they fought with the frantic courage of things more afraid of what stood behind them than what stood before. Their bodies lay twisted in the passageways, eyes glassy, mouths open around teeth too small to look as vicious as they had in life.

Deeper in, the truth waited.

A dragon.

Not one of the great terrors sung by bards, vast as towers and old as kingdoms’ sins. Not a winged doom large enough to shadow a battlefield or break stone walls with one contemptuous sweep of its tail. This one was young. A wyrmling, Gilbert would later learn to call it. Then, he knew only that it was horned, cruel-eyed, and blue.

Memory had lost faces, prices, songs, and the order of small things.

It had not lost the blue.

The color lodged in him more sharply than the frightened face of the man who had sent them down into the dark. It was not the cheerful blue of summer cloth or painted shields. It was storm-blue, lapis darkened toward indigo, the blue-black shine of thunderheads before lightning tears them open. Where torchlight touched its scales, they flashed like polished stone beneath shallow water. Where shadow held them, they looked almost black.

Its hide was smoother than the armor of older dragons Gilbert would later see carved into temple doors and sung into tavern lies, but smooth did not mean soft. Plates overlapped along its neck and shoulders. Smaller scales lay tight across its wedge-shaped head. A single heavy horn jutted forward from its snout, pale against the storm-color of its face, making its silhouette strange in a way no natural beast’s should have been.

Its eyes were worse than its teeth.

Hot. Bright. Insulted.

The chamber where they met it opened wide after the tight passages, and that sudden space was a danger of its own. The roof arched high into darkness. Water moved somewhere below, black and slow, crossed by an old bridge of stone and timber slick with damp. Mineral veins shone faintly in the walls, and their weak glimmer caught along the wyrmling’s scales so that blue light seemed to sleep beneath its hide. The air tasted of wet earth and stone at first.

Then the dragon opened its mouth, and the taste changed.

Dry metal.

Storm dust.

The sharp promise of lightning where lightning had no right to be.

The wyrmling watched them from beyond the bridge.

It looked upon them with the offended arrogance of a thing born believing itself above all others. Its head lowered. Its horn caught the torchlight. Frilled ridges flared along the sides of its skull. Its claws scraped pale lines from the stone with a sound like knives being drawn across bone.

Then it breathed.

Blue-white light crawled between its teeth.

Gilbert remembered the air tightening before the strike. He remembered the hairs rising along his arms beneath mail and cloth. He remembered Dossi swearing, Aaric moving, Oolong’s laughter cutting off at last. The wyrmling’s throat shone from within, not like fire, but like a storm glimpsed through the ribs of a cloud.

Lightning tore across the bridge.

It came in a jagged line, hard and sudden, a piece of open sky forced underground and made angry by the confinement. The world cracked white. Shadows leapt against the cavern walls and vanished. Armor rang. Teeth ached. The old bridge shuddered as blue light struck stone, timber, shield rims, wet boards, and the black water below. Ozone and hot metal filled the chamber so sharply that for a moment Gilbert could not taste his own fear.

Someone shouted.

Someone stumbled.

Gilbert’s shield went numb against his arm, or perhaps his arm went numb beneath the shield. The distinction mattered little. The bridge shook beneath the sudden movement of men trying not to die.

Then the wyrmling came on with claw and fang.

It struck as if the world were expected to make room for it.

Gilbert met it near the center of the bridge.

The first blow nearly drove him to one knee. Claws raked across his shield with a shriek that set his teeth together, the metal still humming faintly from the lightning’s passage. Sparks leapt where talon met iron, bright points in the gloom beneath the lingering stink of storm. He saw his own reflection broken across the gloss of its blue scales, distorted and pale, a young man made smaller by the eye of a dragon.

He struck back.

His blade glanced once, skidding along the hard, smooth plating of its shoulder. The second blow bit. Not deep. Not gloriously. Not in any fashion a bard would have found worth a verse.

But the dragon bled.

That mattered.

The others closed around it.

Dossi’s bow sang from the near side of the bridge, each shot measured, his face gone flat and intent. Aaric moved where the wyrmling was not looking, quick as a thought a man regretted too late. Oolong roared something that might have been encouragement, insult, or both, and hurled himself into the fight with his usual disregard for death’s opinion of the matter. Charlie, stripped of song now, fought beside them, red skin bright against gray stone, black water, and the storm-colored flash of the dragon’s hide.

The dragon was stronger than any one of them.

But they were many, and it was one.

That was a lesson pride learned poorly.

They wounded it quickly. Its scales had not yet grown into legend. Blades found seams. Arrows struck. The beast shrieked, and the sound was too large for its body, a sharp thunder-crack of fury that leapt against the high cavern roof until it seemed a dozen storms answered from the dark. It snapped at Gilbert’s shield and recoiled when Dossi’s arrow struck near one wing. Aaric’s blade opened a line along its flank. Oolong struck it hard enough to make even the dragon notice. Charlie shouted something Gilbert could not remember, and another wound opened.

For the first time, the dragon understood.

It could die.

With a furious beat of its wings, it leapt backward from the bridge and rose above the black water. Wind battered them. Spray lifted from below. Its wings filled the chamber more completely in retreat than they had in attack, sending cloaks snapping and loose dust spinning from the stone. The smell of ozone and scorched rock clung to everything. It retreated not in fear, perhaps, but in the cold calculation of a predator unwilling to trade its life for wounded pride.

Dossi had expected the retreat.

Of course he had.

Before the wyrmling had fully turned, Dossi’s bow was already drawn. His arrow flew clean and struck the beast near the shoulder. The impact twisted it in the air, dropping it a dozen feet before it caught itself with a ragged beat of its wings.

Then Charlie shot.

Gilbert remembered that more clearly than almost anything else in the chamber.

Charlie standing on the bridge, breath hard in his chest, bow lifted. The white of his eyes normal then. Mortal. His fingers released. The arrow vanished into the shadowed air between them and the fleeing wyrmling.

Whether by the will of gods, the turning of the Pattern, or the blind luck that sometimes favored fools and heroes alike, the arrow struck true.

It drove into the dragon’s neck.

For one impossible breath, the blue wyrmling hung above the water, wings spread, body arched against death. Torchlight slid along its storm-dark scales. Its horn pointed toward the cavern roof as if accusing the stone itself. The chamber seemed to hold still. Even the water below appeared to pause, black and waiting.

Then the strength went out of the beast.

It fell.

The crash shook the chamber.

Dark water exploded upward in a spray ten feet high, hammering the underside of the bridge and raining cold over stone, armor, leather, and stunned faces. Ripples slammed against the cavern walls and returned upon themselves. The dragon vanished beneath the surface, rose once in a roll of slack wing and blue-scaled body, then drifted half-submerged near the far stones.

It did not rise again.

Silence followed.

Not peace.

Never peace.

It was the kind of silence that came after something impossible had happened, and the world needed a moment to decide whether it would continue.

The smell of storm faded by degrees into wet stone, black water, and blood.

Gilbert stood on the bridge, breathing hard, shield low, sword wet in his hand. He had killed men before. He had seen beasts slain. He had watched life leave eyes and learned that death was rarely clean, no matter how cleanly men spoke of it afterward.

But this was different.

A dragon lay dead below them.

Young, yes. Small beside the terrors of old tales, yes. A storm not yet grown into its full wrath. A thing that might one day have shattered towers with lightning, turned caravans to smoking ruin, and slept on treasure beneath a desert sky, but had not yet lived long enough to do so.

Still a dragon.

The world had shifted.

Gilbert did not yet know in what direction.

Then Charlie rose into the air.

For a heartbeat Gilbert thought the cavern itself had moved. The black water settled below them too quickly, the ripples drawing inward as if some unseen hand had smoothed them flat. The last echoes of the dragon’s fall faded into the stone and did not return. The silence that followed was not empty.

It listened.

Charlie’s bow lowered in his hand.

He did not lift himself. There was no leap, no command, no flare of will. His boots simply left the bridge, slowly enough that Gilbert saw the space open beneath them and could not make sense of it. Not high. Only a few feet. Enough to make the sight impossible. His arms hung loose at his sides, drifting a little from his body as if the air had taken hold of him. His head tipped back, not in triumph, but as though he were hearing something spoken from above, below, and everywhere at once.

His eyes shone white.

Not bright like flame. Not silver like moonlight on steel. White as a blank page before ink, empty and terrible because Charlie was no longer looking out through them.

No lute sounded. No wind moved. No one spoke.

The cavern, which had just endured battle, breath, wings, arrows, and the death of a dragon, became still in a way no silence after combat could explain. The air pressed against Gilbert’s skin. The hairs on his arms rose beneath mail and cloth.

Dossi lowered his bow but did not loose the arrow he had drawn. Gilbert saw the old archer’s hands hold their discipline by habit alone. There was no target for them. No throat to pierce. No eye to blind. Aaric turned slowly, blade still in hand, his fingers tightening around a weapon that had no answer for what stood before them. Oolong’s mouth opened, and for once the dwarf found no noise large enough to meet the moment.

Gilbert’s shield hand shifted before thought could stop it.

Then it stilled.

He did not know where to put the shield. He could not stand between Charlie and the thing that had taken him. He could not even tell whether Charlie needed protection or whether they did.

When Charlie’s mouth opened, the voice that emerged was not his.

It was not the jovial seller of rolled tobacco. Not the musician who had coaxed quiet from drunk men with nothing more than strings and charm. Not the tiefling who had stood on the bridge and made the shot that dropped a dragon into black water.

It was older than Charlie.

Older, perhaps, than any of them.

It was not loud. That was the wrong word for it. Loudness belonged to bells, thunder, shouting men, and breaking stone. This voice entered the cavern as if it had always been there and they had only now become quiet enough to hear it. It carried distance inside it. Road dust. Sealed doors. Buried law. The weight of words waiting years for mouths.

Charlie spoke.

“As it was, as it is, and as it shall be,
the cycle repeats.

“It is the end of the beginning,
and the beginning of the end.

“The descendant of the Deep Dark has slain the avatar of Roraurim,
who seeks the power of Sharruth,
who was the servant of Tiamat,
who is the servant of Asmodeus,
who is the servant of the Cold One.

“Forsaking the covenant of his blood.

“The first stone has been cast.
The seals of the Daemon Sultan have been weakened.
He shall release Chaos upon this world.

“The avatar of Bahamut, who is the son of Io,
shall travel across time and space,
destroying the seals to create them anew,
maintaining order and upholding the delicate balance of the universe
into eternity.

“The cycle repeats.
As it was, as it is, and as it shall be.

“The first seal has been broken.”

When the last word left him, Charlie fell.

Not far. Only the short distance between omen and stone. Yet he struck the bridge like any mortal man, and whatever had held the cavern in stillness broke at once.

They rushed to him.

Gilbert remembered kneeling beside Charlie. He remembered the cold wet stone beneath one knee and the smell of dragon blood mingled with cavern water. He remembered Oolong’s booming voice gone uncertain, too loud because fear had made him forget how to be quiet. He remembered Dossi checking breath and pulse with hands steadier than his face. He remembered Aaric watching the shadows as if expecting the prophecy itself to grow claws and leap from them.

For several moments, Charlie did not wake.

Those moments stretched longer than the fight.

Then he drew breath.

It came raggedly at first, then again, stronger. His eyes opened. The white light was gone. He looked confused, weak, and far more himself than anything that had spoken through him. Gilbert could not remember whether Charlie asked what had happened. Perhaps he did. Perhaps he claimed not to remember the words. Perhaps he only looked from face to face and knew enough to be afraid.

That part had faded.

The words had not.

The first seal has been broken.

Gilbert had remembered those words more clearly than the battle.

That troubled him too.

Battles blurred. Prophecy endured. A man could forget the frightened face of the villager who first offered coin, forget whether dawn or afternoon had seen them leave the tavern, forget the order of blows on the bridge. But the blue of the dragon and the words spoken in that cavern had remained, sharp and whole, as if carved into the inside of his skull.

At the time, he did not know what they meant.

How could he?

He was a young warrior with a strong arm, a loyal shield, and more courage than understanding. Bahamut, Io, Tiamat, Asmodeus, the Cold One, the Daemon Sultan — these were names too large to fit inside that wet stone chamber where a dead blue wyrmling lay cooling in black water. They belonged to temples, forbidden books, madmen, and the mouths of priests who stared too long at stars.

Yet large things often began in small rooms.

A tavern.

A bridge.

A cave beneath a nameless hill.

A young dragon dead in the shallows.

A tiefling speaking with another voice.

A man named Gilbert de Vere, though that name was not all he was and never had been, standing at the edge of a road he did not yet know he had chosen.

They left that region soon after.

Perhaps there was payment. Perhaps thanks. Perhaps frightened villagers were glad enough to see the danger ended and wise enough not to ask why one of the men who had entered the caves had risen into the air and spoken of broken seals. Gilbert could not recall. Dossi might have. Dossi had always been better at remembering the parts of life that came with coin, witnesses, and obligations.

Gilbert remembered the ship.

Passage bought. Gear carried aboard. Timber creaking beneath foot. Gull cries overhead. Gray water spreading wide beneath a gray sky. Ropes drawn tight. Sailcloth snapping. The smell of tar, salt, river mud, and old fish. Charlie quiet for once. Oolong leaning over the rail as if daring the water to bore him. Aaric watching the shore recede with unreadable eyes. Dossi speaking with the captain, because of course Dossi would know what ought to be asked before men trusted their lives to wood and wind.

Behind them, black water closed over the body of a dragon.

Ahead, the river-roads and sea-lanes bent toward Baldur’s Gate, where cleaner hands would teach Gilbert that rot did not need claws, scales, or teeth to devour a city.
